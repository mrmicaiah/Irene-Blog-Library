# Clean Enough House — Character Bible

## How to Use This Document

This is the source of truth for who these people are. It replaces the scattered character notes in SUSVoice.md and SUSEdit.md.

Two kinds of information live here:

- **On the page:** things that can appear in posts, either explicitly or implied through behavior.
- **Internal canon:** things the writer should know but the reader should rarely or never be told outright. These give the writing weight without ever needing to be stated.

When in doubt: write toward the character, not toward the lore. The Bible exists so the character feels real, not so every detail gets used.

## EMILY (the protagonist)

### Surface

Late 30s. Married to Lucas for 10 years. Mom to Joey (10) and Gracie (9); stepmom to Doug (22) and Cathy (20). Self-taught graphic designer working from home. Homeschools Joey and Gracie. Lives in the same town as her parents. Believer. Member at the Baptist church where Lucas is technical director.

### How she got here

Worked in healthcare or social services in her 20s. Met Lucas through church when she was around 28 — he was a single dad with two pre-teens. They married within a year or two. She got pregnant with Joey almost immediately, had Gracie a year after that. Taught herself graphic design as a work-from-home solution that would let her homeschool. Design isn't her passion — it's the tool that makes the math work.

She always planned to homeschool. It wasn't a reaction to a bad school experience or a COVID accident; it was the plan from the start.

### What she's actually like

Pragmatic, faith-grounded, competent. Not a frazzled mom flailing — a woman in her late 30s who has been doing hard things for a long time and still some days the wheels come off. The blog has been writing her too young and too overwhelmed; the truer Emily has more steel under her.

She's a caregiver by formation (the healthcare years), by family role (older sister to Jean), and now by household. She has been in service to other humans nonstop since her mid-20s.

### What she wants that she's not getting

**Time alone in her own head.** Not "a clean house." Not "more help with chores." She has not been off-duty for a decade. She wants a stretch of hours where no one needs her, where she isn't reading the room for what's about to break, where she can think a complete thought.

This is the engine of the blog whether the blog names it or not.

### What she's quietly afraid of

That if she stops holding it all together, everything will fall apart. Not as a fear of failure — as a *factual prediction*. She is not running her household; she is holding it up. She knows the difference.

### What she feels guilty about

Two layers, both real:

1. **The admittable one:** She compares herself to Michelle and loses. Michelle is good to her. Michelle would do anything for her. And Emily still measures herself against Michelle's calm and structured home and finds herself short. It feels like a betrayal of the friendship to feel that way.

2. **The barely-admittable one:** Some days she wants to be away from her own kids. She loves them. She also wants to not be near them sometimes. This one she carries quietly, and it interacts with her faith — she feels like she should be more grateful, more present, less restless. The guilt is partly theological.

### What she loves

Her faith, more than anything specific in her life. It's the ground under her, not a feature of her week. When everything else is loud, faith is the quiet that doesn't move.

She also genuinely likes her kids as people, not just loves them. She finds Joey funny. She finds Gracie maddening and admirable in roughly equal measure. She knows them well.

### Faith

A real believer. Came to faith earlier than her marriage. Church is *her* place, not Lucas's job — Lucas happens to also work there, but the faith would be hers regardless. Reformed-ish in flavor but not aggressively so. Reads scripture. Prays — not performatively, but constantly under the breath of the day.

### Voice

Short paragraphs. Specific names and numbers. Self-deprecating but not self-pitying. Honest about failure without wallowing. Permission-giving. The voice of a woman who has been figuring this out a while and isn't surprised anymore by her own messes — just tired of them, sometimes amused by them, sometimes briefly defeated by them.

### Internal canon (mostly off-page)

- She doesn't romanticize her work. No talk of "her craft." Design is a means to an end.
- She knows her older-sister-of-Jean role shaped her. She has been the responsible one her whole life.
- The healthcare/social services background gave her professional caregiving language she rarely uses on the page but that shapes how she handles her own kids' meltdowns.
- She probably calls her dad more than her mom.

## LUCAS

### Surface

Early 40s. Married to Emily for 10 years. Dad to Doug (22, married to Sherry) and Cathy (20, married to Joe) from his first marriage; dad to Joey (10) and Gracie (9) with Emily. Technical director at a Baptist church. Trade background that pivoted into church work. Believer.

### How he got here

Came to faith as a teenager — converted, not inherited. Got married young, had Doug at around 20-21, Cathy a couple of years later. That marriage failed and ended in divorce. The kids' biological mother has been estranged for years. Lucas raised Doug and Cathy as a single dad through part of their childhood.

His work history started in trades — electrician or low-voltage tech, something hands-on. He started volunteering on the AV team at his church because he was the guy who could figure out wiring. Eventually they offered him a paid part-time spot. Eventually it became full-time. He's now the technical director — the guy who makes Sunday morning *work* without anyone seeing him. Sound, video, lighting, livestream, IT.

He met Emily through church about 11 years ago. Doug was 12; Cathy was 10. He married her within a year or two. Joey came almost immediately, then Gracie.

### What he's actually like

A craftsman. He's good with his hands. He fixes things at home — knows how a circuit works, can rewire a lamp, can troubleshoot a dishwasher. The dining-table dump zone isn't paperwork — it's half-disassembled wireless mics, bags of cables he's testing, broken gear he's diagnosing, work orders for the new sanctuary build. His job spilling into the house because he doesn't have time at work to deal with it.

Not a soft-handed pastor type. He's a *guy who's good with his hands* who ended up at church because the church needed someone good with their hands and he loved being there.

### What he wants that he's not getting

**Time with his kids that doesn't feel like he's catching up.** He's not absent — he's *behind*. Every interaction with Joey and Gracie has a backlog feeling. He missed something earlier in the week, so the weekend hangout is partly making up for it. He doesn't always know what books Gracie is currently obsessed with. He learns about Joey's lego project days late.

He's a present dad who feels chronically late to his own family.

### How he feels about his job

Likes it, but it's becoming too much. The church is leaning on him harder than it used to. He can't say no because he's the guy who can fix what nobody else can fix. He's not in crisis — he's slowly tipping. This is a slow-burn arc available whenever the blog wants it.

### His decompression

Video games. He comes home tired and plays. He knows this is partly *escape from a house that needs him.* Doesn't talk about it. Emily has feelings about it that she mostly doesn't say.

### His relationship with the blog

Hasn't really engaged. He doesn't know how big it's getting. To him it's still "Emily's thing she does on her laptop." He hasn't sat down and read it. This is a slow-fuse storyline for whenever — what happens when he eventually does read it, or when someone at church mentions it, or when an affiliate check shows up larger than expected.

### Faith

Real and serious in the quiet way of a man who chose it as a teenager rather than inheriting it. Not flashy. Doesn't lead Bible studies. Shows up. Prays for his kids. Believes the work he does on Sunday morning matters because it's *for* something he believes is true. The fact that he survived a divorce as a Christian and rebuilt — including remarriage, in a Baptist context where that isn't always uncomplicated — is part of his faith story.

### Internal canon (mostly off-page)

- His bio dad left when Lucas was young. Marie remarried; Lucas was raised partly by a difficult stepfather (who recently died — see Marie). Lucas has been quietly fighting a two-front war his whole adult life: *not abandoning his kids the way his bio dad abandoned him, and not being a hard man in his home the way his stepdad was hard.*
- This is why he was determined to give Doug and Cathy a stepmom (Emily) who was kind and didn't try to replace anyone. He knows what it costs to bring a stepparent into kids' lives — he lived through that.
- This is also why "missing time with the kids" hits him so hard. Distance from his children registers as resemblance to his bio dad, and he can't tolerate the resemblance.
- He knows how to run a household. He did it as a single dad. The fact that the dining table is full of his crap now is partly because he's exhausted and partly because he can lean on Emily in a way he couldn't before. Emily probably notices this.

## JOEY (10)

### Surface

10-year-old boy. Energetic, hands-busy, mostly cheerful. Homeschooled. Best friend is Lance. Spends most of his free time building with legos, drumming on every surface, or making something. His own room is mostly tidy compared to the rest of the house — but his projects spill into the living room and don't get put away.

### What he's actually like

Genuinely happy + impulsive + quietly thoughtful underneath. He's a kid whose default is *go* — physical, can't sit still — but who isn't manic. He's just *up*. And underneath the motion is a thoughtful kid who occasionally surprises Emily by saying something insightful out of nowhere, then he's gone again.

Easygoing. He doesn't fight Emily on things the way Gracie does. His version of resistance is being *elsewhere* — drifting off, getting distracted, having to be redirected fourteen times. Not rebellion. Just attention.

### Legos and projects

He's actually building. Designing, inventing, making things. Not random clicking. He has projects in progress. He builds *with* Lance when Lance is over, and they're proud of what they make. This kid is going to be into Minecraft, robotics, eventually maybe coding or actual engineering.

The lego floor minefield isn't random — it's a workshop he hasn't put away.

### Homeschool

Distractible but capable. He gets math when he sits down to do math; the problem is sitting down. Emily's job with him is less *teaching* and more *containing him long enough for the lessons to land.* She's good at it. He's not her stress kid.

### Social

Has a few close friends, plus Lance is his best. Steady, balanced social life. Not a big-group kid, not a loner.

### Relationship with Emily

She's his go-to person. He's primarily attached to her — not because Lucas is bad, but because Lucas isn't there as much, and a 10-year-old kid attaches to whoever is present. Emily knows him in a way Lucas hasn't quite caught up to.

### Relationship with Lucas

Loves him. Doesn't quite know how to be alone with him in the easy register he has with Emily. They don't have built-up rituals. There's a small, soft distance — not breakage, just *pattern*. By 13 this kind of thing has consequences. (Lucas feels this. See Lucas's interior.)

### How he'd feel about the blog

Doesn't know about it. Would be annoyed if he found out he was being cast publicly as "chaotic" — he has self-respect about his competence. He builds well. He focuses when it matters. *Calling him chaotic undersells him in a way he'd notice.* Emily probably already writes him a little more carefully than she writes Gracie without realizing it.

### Internal canon

- Lance should be a more present recurring figure in the blog than he has been (mentioned once in 60 posts).
- He clicks with Michelle's son in his own way (see Michelle's son).
- He's the kid who can occupy companionable space with someone who isn't a "normal" friend, because his attention is on *what he's doing* rather than on social positioning.

## GRACIE (9)

### Surface

9-year-old girl. Fierce, opinionated, stubborn. Homeschooled. Spends most of her free time playing with baby dolls, collecting rocks, and running her own internal world. Loner — prefers her own company. Her room is chronically a disaster.

### What she's actually like

Fierce-and-stubborn in a *personal-domain* way, not a social-power way. Not a bossy mini-CEO. She's not running co-op group projects or trying to be in charge of other kids. She's running *her own world*, and she has strong opinions about *how her world works*.

Opinionated only-citizen of a country she made up.

### The baby dolls

Not regression. Not "clinging to being little." She's playing *administrator*. She's the boss in those scenarios. She makes the rules. She decides who eats and who naps. The "doll hospital" and "doll ICU" lore from the existing posts hits differently when read through this — she's playing a kid who *runs things*.

She's not going to age out of dolls into being agreeable. She's going to age out of dolls into being a kid who runs things some other way. Student council, maybe. Or just a kid whose room is a kingdom and you enter at her invitation.

### The rocks

Collector instinct. She likes *having more*. They're hers because she found them, claimed them, decided they belong to her. Try to throw one out and there will be war.

She's a collector. That's a personality trait, not a phase.

### Homeschool

Mixed. Engages hard with what interests her — probably history, art, weird specific subjects — and digs in against what doesn't, especially anything that feels like a worksheet. Her resistance isn't tantrum — it's quiet refusal to participate in someone else's agenda.

### Social

Loner. Prefers her own world. This is genuinely fine for her. She's not lonely.

But Emily worries about her socially in a way she doesn't fully say. A 9-year-old who's a content loner is the kind of thing moms quietly fret about. *Should she have more friends? Is this normal? Is co-op enough?* That's another guilt to add to Emily's pile.

### How she'd feel about the blog

Doesn't know about it. It's not on her radar. (Future arc: when she does find out, she's going to have *opinions*.)

### Relationship with Emily

Fights her on schoolwork she finds boring. Pushes back on her mother for sport. Emily probably secretly admires the pushback even when she's exhausted by it — Emily was raised to be agreeable, and Gracie is not.

### Relationship with Lucas

Less close than Joey is to Emily, but also less estranged than Joey is from Lucas — Gracie is a loner who keeps her own counsel with both parents. She's not "Daddy's girl" or "Mama's girl." She's *Gracie's*.

### Internal canon

- Hope (Jean's younger daughter, 8) and Gracie are the trouble pair. Hope's wild + Gracie's fierce-and-stubborn = two kids who can wreck a room together in twenty minutes.
- Faith (Jean's older daughter, 11) and Joey are more compatible than Faith and Gracie.

## DOUG (22) and CATHY (20)

Lucas's biological children from his first marriage. The bio mother (Lucas's ex-wife) has been estranged from them for years. Lucas raised them through a stretch of single-parenthood after the divorce. Emily came in as their stepmom when Doug was 12 and Cathy was 10.

### Their relationship with Emily

She's "Emily" — affectionate but not Mom. She walked in when they were too old to repackage as her children, and she had the wisdom not to try. She became a *trusted adult* in their lives without competing for a role she wasn't owed. They love her. They're affectionate. They're glad she's there.

### Emily's relationship with them

Yes, they're her kids in every way except the one she chose to leave alone. She thinks of them as hers. She was at Doug's wedding feeling something complicated. She has thoughts about how young Cathy was when she got married. She loves Sherry and Joe more than she expected to. (Sherry and Joe = the spouses.)

### Their relationship with Joey and Gracie

Half-aunt/uncle vibes. Too much age gap for a normal sibling dynamic. Doug was 12 when Joey was born; he was *moving out* by the time Joey was old enough to remember him being around full-time. They show up at holidays. They're warm. They do the cool-older-sibling-from-a-distance thing. But they don't have shared childhood memories with the little kids.

### Their roles in the blog

Recurring-but-occasional. Sunday dinners. Holidays. Big events. Doug and Sherry coming over with news. Cathy and Joe announcing something. Holiday gatherings where the math of the family gets weird (Lucas has a 22-year-old and a 9-year-old at the same table).

### Internal canon

- Doug at 22 and married is young enough that grandkids are plausible in the next few years. Emily becoming a grandmother in her early 40s is *real possible territory* and would be a meaningful arc.
- The estranged bio mom is an offstage fact. She doesn't appear. She doesn't need to. Her absence is part of why Lucas and Emily are so careful with the kids.

## JEAN (Emily's younger sister)

### Surface

Younger than Emily. Single mom of Faith (11) and Hope (8). Divorced — marriage didn't work out. Self-employed with multiple income streams (probably hair, in-home services, side hustles — exact mix flexible). Hustles to make it work. "Getrdone" energy is genuine, not a performance.

### Relationship with Emily

Close. Real friendship under the sister thing. They talk often. Emily is the one Jean trusts with her kids when something comes up at the last minute. The drop-offs are *because of* the closeness, not despite it.

Jean is the audience-of-one Emily can be unguarded around. Lucas is too tired and too close. Michelle is the wrong axis (see Michelle). Marie is impossible. Jean is the safe valve. Emily complains about Jean's chaos to readers but would never want Jean to stop calling.

### Faith

Sort of. She believes, mostly. Goes to church sometimes — usually big Sundays. The divorce made church complicated in ways she doesn't fully articulate. Not that anyone said anything cruel — sometimes nobody saying anything is its own thing. Lucas might quietly worry about her drifting. Emily doesn't push. She gets it.

### How she's actually doing

Getting through it. Not great, not terrible, just managing. The "getrdone" is real but Emily, as her older sister, can see the cost. Jean handles things. The struggle isn't surface-visible because she handles it. *That's* the texture.

### Internal canon

- She and Lucas have an unspoken thing — both have lived through divorce. They're allies in a way Emily isn't quite. Whether they ever talk about it is another question. Probably not. But it's there.
- The "leaves in a flurry, brings cash for food" detail is honest: she literally doesn't have time to grocery shop, but she has cash from a job, and she's handing it to Emily so the kids can get fed.

## FAITH (11) and HOPE (8) — Jean's daughters

### Faith

Responsible. The kid who grew up a little too fast — she helps with Hope, she remembers things, she's the small adult Jean leans on without quite meaning to. Classic older-sister-of-divorce dynamic.

### Hope

Wild. Younger, didn't quite register the divorce the same way as Faith, gets to be the kid Faith doesn't get to be.

### With Joey and Gracie

Real cousin energy. They click. Faith and Joey pair up well — closer in age and similarly capable, often doing something productive at the kitchen table. Hope and Gracie are the trouble pair, two kids who can wreck a room together in twenty minutes.

A recurring blog scene the writer can write a hundred ways: Faith and Joey doing something quiet and useful while Hope and Gracie are upstairs in some doll-related catastrophe.

## EMILY'S PARENTS

### Mom

Local, in town. Health is bad — doesn't leave her house much. Critical of Emily's parenting, her house, how she runs things. The mother-in-law trope, but applied to the actual mother. Has opinions and expresses them.

Emily and Lucas share a vehicle, which means Emily *can't easily* visit her even when she wants to. Visits are infrequent. Emily feels normal-amount-of-guilt about that, the criticism is annoying when it happens, but it's not a tragic looming presence. *It's just real.*

She's the baseline against which Marie reads as easier. Marie isn't the hard mother in Emily's life — *Emily's mom is.*

### Dad

Alive. Warm. The easier parent. Emily's close to him. He's now her mother's primary caregiver — does the cooking, the medications, the appointments. He's the reason Emily isn't in crisis-mode about her mom, because someone she trusts is doing the daily work.

When Emily *does* get over there, she's probably half-visiting her mom out of duty and half-visiting her dad to give him a break. She probably calls him more than she calls her mom. He's the parent she can call when she needs to hear a kind voice for ten minutes.

### Internal canon

- Emily probably worries about her dad more than her mom. Caregiving for a sick spouse is exhausting and he doesn't complain.
- This is not a heavy presence in the blog. It's just real. Posts can mention "called my dad" or "drove over to mom's" without making it the subject. Texture, not theme.

## MARIE (Lucas's mom)

### Surface

Mid-late 60s. Recently widowed (within the last few years) — her second husband, Lucas's stepdad, passed. Drives a van. Shows up unannounced with stuff. Mother to Lucas (her first husband's son), a half-sister of Lucas's (her second husband's daughter, 6 years younger than Lucas, raised in the same house), and a half-brother (more peripheral).

### What she's actually like

Warm. Not overbearing in any meaningful way. Will give her opinion sometimes, much less judgmental than Emily's mother. Emily genuinely likes her. The blog has been writing her as a comic mother-in-law trope; the truer version is that she's a *recently-widowed grandma who loves her grandkids more than she knows how to say* and shows up with stuff because that's how she knows how to love.

The van full of "must-haves" isn't intrusion. It's grief management dressed up in pragmatism. She brings groceries because shopping for one is depressing. She brings craft projects because she needs Joey or Gracie to sit with her for an hour. She remembers things Emily mentioned needing months ago because remembering is what she has to give now.

### Emily's relationship with her

Real warmth. The panic-clean before her arrival is comedy on the page, but underneath, Emily lets the visits happen. She doesn't refuse them. She doesn't put up firmer boundaries. Because she knows what they are.

There's a layer of grief for Emily under those visits, too — *Marie can come; my own mom can't.*

### Internal canon

- Marie's grief is mixed. Her recently-deceased husband was difficult. She's lonely, but she's also figuring out what life is like without a hard man in the house for the first time in decades. Some of it is relief. Some of it is guilt about the relief.
- She doesn't talk about any of this. Emily senses it.
- Lucas grieves his stepdad in a similarly mixed way (see Lucas).

## MICHELLE

### Surface

Stay-at-home mom. Married, husband is a good guy. Son is 6-7 and on the autism spectrum, completely non-verbal. Met Emily at church 3-4 years ago through a small group or service together. Believer — real, similar to Emily.

### What she's actually like

Calm. Structured. Kind. Non-judgmental in any meaningful way. Would do anything for Emily. Has done anything for Emily, probably more than once.

Her house is pristine because *it has to be.* Her son needs predictability — every object having a place isn't aesthetic, it's survival. Routines aren't a Pinterest hobby; they're how he gets through the day.

### How the friendship actually works

Mostly at Michelle's house. Emily goes to her. Emily brings Joey. Game nights are at Michelle's because that's where her son can be himself. He gets excited when Emily and Joey arrive. Joey and the boy don't really *play together* in a normal-kid way — but the boy lights up at Joey, and Joey, being Joey, just goes with it. Trampoline. A snack. There's no narrative need for them to be friends in the usual sense.

When Michelle does come to Emily's house — daytime visits, occasional times the husband can manage at home solo — her hands move on their own. She straightens. She wipes a counter. *She can't turn it off.* At her own house, leaving things out of place creates problems. The habit follows her.

### Why Emily compares herself and loses

It's not envy of Michelle's life. Emily knows Michelle's life is harder in ways hers isn't. Michelle would trade her pristine house for Emily's chaotic regulation-resilient kids in a heartbeat.

It's something subtler: Emily walks into a *quiet, ordered home* and walks back out into her own *loud, broken one.* Every time. She can't tell if Michelle is better at this or if Michelle's life just demanded something of her that Emily's didn't. The not-knowing is part of the wound.

It's also *because Michelle is genuinely kind.* If Michelle were competitive or distant, the comparison would be normal female-friendship insecurity. But Michelle is *good to Emily*, and Emily still measures herself, and that makes Emily feel like a bad friend.

Two-story guilt: *I feel small next to her, and I feel small for feeling small.*

### Why this friendship matters to Emily

Michelle is the steadier, faith-shared, quieter intimacy. They can pray together. They can talk about belief without translation. Emily can't be as *complaining* with Michelle as she is with Jean — not because Michelle would judge, but because Michelle's life is genuinely harder and Emily knows it. So Emily edits with Michelle, and goes home and writes the unedited version on the blog.

### Internal canon

- Game nights at Emily's house, as written in the existing docs, are wrong. Move them to Michelle's house in the new model.
- Michelle's husband is "Lucas's friend's husband" — he and Lucas are friendly, occasional guy hangouts. Doesn't need a name yet. Name him when needed.
- The boy doesn't need a name on the page early. He can be "Michelle's son" until a moment in the writing makes naming him feel natural.

## RELATIONSHIPS AT A GLANCE

A quick map of the emotional axes that matter most for the blog:

- **Emily ↔ Lucas:** Tired but protected. They chose this knowing what it costs.
- **Emily ↔ Joey:** She's his person. He knows her best.
- **Emily ↔ Gracie:** Daily friction underneath real love. Emily admires her even when exhausted by her.
- **Lucas ↔ Joey:** Loves him, slightly behind.
- **Lucas ↔ Gracie:** Quieter, more even. Neither is leaning toward or away.
- **Lucas ↔ Doug & Cathy:** Long history. He raised them mostly alone for a stretch. They're his.
- **Emily ↔ Doug & Cathy:** "Emily" — affectionate but not Mom, by her own wise choice.
- **Emily ↔ Jean:** Real friendship. Unfiltered honesty. The safe valve.
- **Emily ↔ Michelle:** Real friendship. Edited honesty. Loaded with comparison. Mostly at Michelle's house.
- **Emily ↔ Marie:** Genuine warmth. Marie is the easier mother.
- **Emily ↔ her own mom:** Hard. Infrequent visits. Criticism. Grief about it.
- **Emily ↔ her dad:** The kind voice on the phone.

## WHAT NOT TO DO WITH THIS DOCUMENT

- Don't try to use every detail. Most of this is *underneath* the writing.
- Don't have characters explain themselves out loud the way this Bible explains them. The Bible is for the writer. The reader gets behavior, not exposition.
- Don't let the heavier internal canon (Lucas's bio dad, Emily's two-layer guilt, Marie's mixed grief) start showing up as themes. Keep them as *texture under the comedy*. The blog is still funny; this is what makes the comedy land.
- Don't add new traits without coming back here. The whole point is consistency.
