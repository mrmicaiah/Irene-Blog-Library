# Clean Enough House - Post Type Templates

This document defines the formats available in each of the two publishing slots. The slots themselves are described in SUSMaster.md.

- **Anchor (Tuesday, 9am CT)** — 1,200–2,500 words. Substantial. Affiliate-friendly. Seven formats below.
- **Lighter (Thursday, 9am CT)** — 600–900 words typical. Variety slot. Seven formats below.

Slot assignment isn't strict. A Product Roundup could plausibly go in either slot if the moment fits. The default is Anchor types in the Tuesday slot and Lighter types in the Thursday slot.

---

## ANCHOR FORMATS

| Type | Purpose | Typical Length | Affiliate Focus |
|------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Disaster Chronicle | Story + lesson | 1,400–1,800 words | Up to 2 products |
| Method Tutorial | Deep teaching | 1,600–2,000 words | Up to 2 products |
| Zone Transformation | Before/after journey | 1,500–1,900 words | Up to 2 products |
| Product Roundup | Affiliate-heavy | 1,200–1,600 words | 2 products max |
| Quick Win | Fast tactic | 800–1,200 words | 0–1 products |
| Myth Buster | Why advice fails | 1,300–1,700 words | Up to 2 products |
| System Breakdown | Comprehensive guide | 2,000–2,500 words | Up to 2 products |

**Affiliate hard cap:** No anchor post contains more than 2 products. This is a global rule, not per-format.

---

### ANCHOR 1: THE DISASTER CHRONICLE

**Purpose:** Tell the story of a specific disaster and the lesson or method that emerged from it. Most entertaining format, builds character connection.

**Structure:**

1. **The Setup (150–200 words)** — Open in the middle of chaos. Specific time. Trigger event. Stakes.
2. **The Disaster Unfolds (200–300 words)** — Play-by-play. Failed attempts. Peak disaster moment.
3. **The Method That Saved Me (300–400 words)** — Which method, step-by-step, why it worked here.
4. **The Products That Helped (150–200 words, optional)** — Up to 2 products with affiliate links + disclaimer.
5. **The Takeaway (100–150 words)** — What Emily learned. One actionable next step for the reader.

**Total:** 1,400–1,800 words.

---

### ANCHOR 2: THE METHOD TUTORIAL

**Purpose:** Deep teaching of one method from Emily's Battle Plan (SUSFramework.md). Educational focus, still relatable.

**Structure:**

1. **Why This Method Exists (150–200 words)** — The specific problem. Who it helps.
2. **How I Discovered This (200–250 words)** — The failure that led to the method. The "aha."
3. **The Method Step-by-Step (400–500 words)** — Numbered steps, clear instructions, time estimates, pitfalls.
4. **Real-Life Application (200–300 words)** — Two or three scenarios where Emily uses it. Good days vs. hard days.
5. **Tools & Products (150–200 words, optional)** — Up to 2 products + disclaimer.
6. **Your Turn (100–150 words)** — One specific challenge for the reader this week.

**Total:** 1,600–2,000 words.

---

### ANCHOR 3: THE ZONE TRANSFORMATION

**Purpose:** Before-and-after of conquering a specific area. Visual journey. Affiliate potential.

**Structure:**

1. **The Battle Zone (100–150 words)** — Which area, why it's chaos, the emotional weight.
2. **Before: The Full Truth (200–250 words)** — Honest description. How long. What Emily avoided because of it.
3. **The Strategy (300–400 words)** — Which methods used. Why this approach. The plan.
4. **The Process (300–400 words)** — Step-by-step what happened. Surprises. Real interruptions.
5. **The Solutions (200–300 words, optional)** — Up to 2 products + disclaimer.
6. **After & Maintenance (150–200 words)** — Current state. How Emily sustains it. Honest sustainability.

**Total:** 1,500–1,900 words.

---

### ANCHOR 4: THE PRODUCT ROUNDUP

**Purpose:** Solving one specific organizing problem with product recommendations.

**Structure:**

1. **The Problem (150–200 words)** — One specific pain point. Why it's hard. Failed attempts.
2. **What I Needed (100–150 words)** — Criteria for solutions. Budget.
3. **The Products (500–600 words)** — 2 products max, each with: what it is, why she got it, how she uses it, scenario it solves, honest pros/cons, price. **Disclaimer at top of section.**
4. **The Setup (150–200 words)** — How Emily implemented. Time. Current state.

**Note:** This format used to allow 5–8 products. The new 2-product cap means a "roundup" is really now a deep dive on two solutions. Adjust accordingly.

**Per-product format:**

```
**[Product Name](affiliate-link)** ($XX)
What it is: [One sentence]
Why I got it: [Specific problem]
How I use it: [Actual application]
Pros: [2–3 honest benefits]
Cons: [1–2 real drawbacks]
Verdict: [Worth it? For whom?]
```

**Total:** 1,200–1,600 words.

---

### ANCHOR 5: THE QUICK WIN

**Purpose:** Fast tactic for immediate relief. The shortest anchor format.

**Structure:**

1. **The Crisis (100–150 words)** — Urgent scenario. Time constraint. Panic level: high.
2. **The Quick Fix (200–300 words)** — One specific tactic. Max 5 steps. Exact time commitment.
3. **The Results (100–150 words)** — What this buys you. How long it lasts. When to use this vs. a deeper method.
4. **Optional: One Quick Product (100–150 words)** — One product max, plus disclaimer if used.

**Total:** 800–1,200 words.

---

### ANCHOR 6: THE MYTH BUSTER

**Purpose:** Why popular organizing advice fails in real chaos. Builds trust through contrast.

**Structure:**

1. **The Popular Advice (100–150 words)** — What "they" say. Where Emily heard it. Why it sounds good.
2. **Why I Tried It (150–200 words)** — Emily's hope for it. How she implemented. Initial optimism.
3. **The Reality (300–400 words)** — What actually happened. Where it broke down. Specific examples — the Gracie/Lucas/Joey factor.
4. **What Actually Works Instead (300–400 words)** — Emily's alternative. Which method. Why it fits real life.
5. **The Products (150–200 words, optional)** — Up to 2 products for the method that *does* work + disclaimer.

**Voice note for this format:** Not mean about the original advice. "Works for some people, not for me" — specific about *why* it failed.

**Total:** 1,300–1,700 words.

---

### ANCHOR 7: THE SYSTEM BREAKDOWN

**Purpose:** Comprehensive guide to implementing one complete system. Deep dive. Use occasionally.

**Structure:**

1. **The System Overview (150–200 words)** — Which method. What problem it solves. Who needs this.
2. **My Journey With This (200–300 words)** — When Emily discovered the need. Failed attempts.
3. **Building the System (500–600 words)** — Phase 1: Planning. Phase 2: Setup. Phase 3: Implementation. Phase 4: Troubleshooting.
4. **The Tools & Products (200–300 words, optional)** — Up to 2 products + disclaimer.
5. **Maintaining It (200–250 words)** — Daily/weekly maintenance. How it handles kid chaos. Honest sustainability.
6. **Is This For You? (100–150 words)** — Who this helps. When to try it. Simpler alternatives if too big.

**Total:** 2,000–2,500 words.

## LIGHTER FORMATS

| Type | Purpose | Typical Length | Notes |
|------|---------|----------------|-------|
| Field Notes | Vignette from the week | 600–900 words | No teaching required |
| Q&A | Reader question + answer | 600–900 words | Builds parasocial trust |
| List with Personality | The form does the work | 500–800 words | Voice-heavy |
| Status Report | Arc check-in | 600–900 words | Pays off planted threads |
| The Observation | One small idea, tight | 500–800 words | Most distilled format |
| Letter | To someone or something | 600–900 words | Different emotional register |
| Photo Walk | Zone narration | 600–900 words | Requires actual photos |

**Lighter posts are allowed to teach but not required to.** A Lighter that lands on a method tip in the last paragraph is fine. A Lighter that's pure character observation with no takeaway is also fine. The point of the slot is *variety* — not a smaller version of an Anchor.

**Affiliate posture for Lighter:** Default to none. If a product comes up organically in the moment, one product with a single link is acceptable. Don't structure a Lighter around products — that's a Roundup, which goes in Anchor.

---

### LIGHTER 1: FIELD NOTES

**Purpose:** A specific moment from the week. No method, no takeaway, no fix. Just a scene observed honestly.

**Structure:** Looser than anchors. Roughly:

1. **The scene** — One specific moment, dropped in cold. Time, place, who's there.
2. **What happened** — Played out small. Details over summary.
3. **What it meant, or didn't** — A short reflection if there is one. A shrug if there isn't. Not every moment has a lesson.

**Voice note:** This is the format closest to journaling. Specific over universal. The point isn't "what readers can learn from this" — the point is "this was a real moment in Emily's actual life."

**Example seed:** "Gracie spent forty minutes arguing with a baby doll about screen time. I didn't intervene. I had questions."

**Total:** 600–900 words.

---

### LIGHTER 2: Q&A

**Purpose:** A reader question (real, or honestly invented as a stand-in for a question Emily actually gets) plus her answer. Builds parasocial trust by showing how she thinks.

**Structure:**

1. **The question** — Quoted in full or summarized. If invented, frame it honestly: "A reader-shaped question I've been getting in some form for months..." Don't fake reader names.
2. **The honest answer** — The way Emily would actually answer a friend asking this. Caveats included. Permission-giving.
3. **The aside, if any** — A shorter follow-up thought, a related point, a "but also..."

**Voice note:** Not advice-column-prim. Not "here are 5 tips." Conversational. If the answer is "I don't know," that's a real answer.

**Example seed:** "How do you handle it when your husband doesn't help with the housework?" (Emily's honest answer involves Lucas's actual job, the trade-offs of the marriage, and the acknowledgment that she's not always sure she handles it well.)

**Total:** 600–900 words.

---

### LIGHTER 3: LIST WITH PERSONALITY

**Purpose:** A form that does the heavy lifting. The list itself is the joke or the insight. Items are short, voice carries it.

**Structure:**

1. **The setup** — One short paragraph that frames the list. (Example: "Things I've hidden in the oven this month, ranked by how clean the oven was when I closed it on top of them.")
2. **The list** — 5–10 items. Each item is one or two sentences. Specificity is the rule — "a casserole dish with the lid on" beats "some dishes."
3. **The button** — A single closing line that lands.

**Voice note:** This is one of the few places the blog earns the right to be funny without anchoring the comedy in a deeper truth. The form is allowed to be the point. But honest specificity — real items, real moments — is what keeps it from feeling like a generic listicle.

**Example seeds:**
- "Things Joey has used as a drum surface, ranked"
- "Six things I've hidden in the oven this month"
- "Reasons Gracie cried this week, in chronological order"
- "What's actually on my dining table right now"

**Total:** 500–800 words.

---

### LIGHTER 4: STATUS REPORT

**Purpose:** A check-in on a recurring arc or thread (see SUSArcs.md). The post that pays off something planted weeks or months ago.

**Structure:**

1. **The check-in framing** — One short paragraph. ("Lucas brought home another box of cables this week. The dining table situation has officially entered Phase 4.")
2. **The current state** — What's happening now. Specific.
3. **What's changed since last time** — If readers have been tracking this, what's new? If not, light enough that new readers can follow.
4. **What's next, or what isn't** — Where this seems to be heading. Or honest acknowledgment that it isn't heading anywhere yet.

**Voice note:** This format is for arcs that have built up enough background to deserve a beat. A Status Report on an arc that's been seeded once is premature — it'll feel like a recap of nothing. Wait for the arc to actually have something to report.

**Example seeds:**
- "Where things stand with Lucas's church job" (Arc 1)
- "An update on the doll hospital" (Arc 2)
- "We almost bought a second car this week" (Arc 6)
- "Joey asked Lucas to help with something" (Arc 7)

**Total:** 600–900 words.

---

### LIGHTER 5: THE OBSERVATION

**Purpose:** One small idea, expressed tightly. The most distilled format on the blog.

**Structure:**

1. **The hook** — One paragraph that lands the idea, often as a small admission or a moment of noticing.
2. **The unfolding** — Three or four short paragraphs that work the idea out. Examples, slight pushback, refinements.
3. **The landing** — A short closing thought that doesn't try to wrap things in a bow.

**Voice note:** This is the format closest to an essay. The bar is the *idea* — if there isn't a real idea worth the post, don't write one. "I figured out why I keep cleaning the same three surfaces. It's not the surfaces" is a workable seed; "cleaning is hard" is not.

**Example seeds:**
- "I figured out why I keep cleaning the same three surfaces"
- "The version of me my kids see is the version that matters"
- "Most of what I call 'organizing' is actually grief"
- "I don't want a clean house. I want a quiet half-hour."

**Total:** 500–800 words.

---

### LIGHTER 6: LETTER

**Purpose:** A device that licenses a different emotional register. Addressed to a person, an object, a former self, a future kid.

**Structure:** Letter-shaped. Salutation. Body. Sign-off. Doesn't need to be strict — it can drift between actual letter and reflection — but the letter framing should hold.

**Voice note:** This format is the one that can dip into more vulnerable registers without it feeling out of nowhere — because letters are private, and reading one feels like overhearing. Use sparingly. A blog that runs a letter every other Thursday loses the device.

**Example seeds:**
- "To Past Emily, who thought matching baskets would solve it"
- "To Lucas's dining table, on the occasion of being clear for an entire afternoon"
- "To Gracie, when she's old enough to have a daughter of her own"
- "To the version of Marie who used to come over with the van full of stuff she didn't know she was bringing for herself"

**Total:** 600–900 words. Keep it shorter than that if the letter wants to be shorter.

---

### LIGHTER 7: PHOTO WALK

**Purpose:** Walk through one zone in its actual current state, narrated honestly. The photos are non-negotiable for this format — without them it's just a Field Notes post.

**Structure:**

1. **The setup** — Why this zone, why now, what state it's in.
2. **Photo + paragraph + photo + paragraph** — Walk the room, photographing as you go. Each photo gets a short narration.
3. **The honest assessment** — What this zone tells Emily about the week, the season, the household.

**Voice note:** This format only works if Emily takes real photos of the actual mess. A staged photo walk is dishonest and the reader will feel it.

**Affiliate note:** A Photo Walk *can* organically include a product (Emily walks past a basket she actually uses, names it, links it), but the post is not structured around products.

**Example seeds:**
- "What my office looks like the day after a deadline"
- "A photo walk through Gracie's room on a Tuesday morning"
- "The kitchen, with no edits, at 4pm on a Friday"

**Total:** 600–900 words.

## Universal Elements (Both Slots)

### Voice
Always Dana White-calibrated for late-30s Emily (see SUSVoice.md). Specific over abstract. Names of family members. Concrete numbers and times. Self-deprecating without self-pity.

### Characters
Anyone in a post is who they are in SUSCharacters.md. If a character does something inconsistent with the Bible, it's wrong. If the user wants to evolve a character, that's a conversation, not an in-post decision.

### Arc/Thread Touches
Whenever organic, touch an arc or thread (see SUSArcs.md). A Lighter post that name-drops Sherry, a phone call to Dad, a Marie drop-by — these touches keep the world dense without doing arc-shaped work.

### Affiliate Disclaimer (Standard)

Use this exact disclaimer at the start of any product section:

```
*This post contains affiliate links, which means I earn a small commission if you purchase through these links, at no extra cost to you. I only recommend products I actually use in my own chaotic household. Your support helps keep this blog running—thank you!*
```

### Endings
Anchor posts get an actionable takeaway or clear next step. Lighter posts don't have to. Some Lighter posts end with a shrug, and that's the right ending.
