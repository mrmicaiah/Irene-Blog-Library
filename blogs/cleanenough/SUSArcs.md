# Systems Under Siege — Arcs & Threads

## How to Use This Document

This is the live storyline tracker. It exists so the blog has continuity — so a reader who's been here six months feels like they know these people, not like they're reading 60 unrelated posts.

Two kinds of through-lines live here:

- **Arcs** have shape. They have a beginning, a middle, and an eventual ending. They develop over time. Eventually they resolve, and a new arc takes their place. Arcs are tracked by *phase* (where are we in this story right now?) and have *open threads* (what's unfinished, what's been planted that hasn't paid off yet).

- **Threads** don't resolve. They're always present. They give the blog texture and emotional consistency without going anywhere in particular. Threads are tracked by *current state* and *recent appearances*.

The distinction matters because:

- An arc that never moves is a problem. A thread that never moves is the point.
- Arcs need pacing — too many beats too fast and it stops feeling real. Too few and readers lose the thread.
- Threads need *touches*, not beats. A line about Emily's faith. A reference to the shared car. The kids' grandparents on the phone. They keep the world dense.

When writing a post, ask: does this post move an arc, touch a thread, or do neither? All three are valid. But if a post would naturally touch a thread and it doesn't, the post is thinner than it needed to be.

## ACTIVE ARCS

### ARC 1: Lucas's Job Becoming Too Much

**Status:** Slow burn. Beat 0 — established but unmoved.

**The shape:** Lucas is the technical director at a Baptist church and is the guy who makes Sunday morning *work* without anyone seeing him. The church leans on him harder than it used to. He can't say no because he's the only one who can fix what he fixes. The arc moves him from *quietly tipping* → *visibly struggling* → *something has to change* → *something does* (or doesn't, and the cost shows). This is years of story, not months.

**Where we are:** Established as fact in the world. Hasn't moved on the page. Dining table is full of his work spillover. He decompresses with video games. Emily has feelings she mostly doesn't say.

**Open threads:**
- The dining table as visual proxy for his job spilling into the house
- A specific build/event at the church that's the first crack
- A late-night conversation that names the problem out loud
- Whether Emily ever asks him to consider stepping back
- The version where he doesn't step back, and Emily adjusts to that being the new normal

**How it pays off:** Probably never as a single dramatic moment. More likely a long arc of accumulation, then a quiet decision (or non-decision) months from now. The reader who's been here for that whole stretch will feel it. New readers won't notice it's there.

**What to avoid:** Making it the subject of a post too early. This arc gets *touched*, not *featured*, until it's been touched enough that featuring it has weight.

### ARC 2: Gracie Aging Out of Rocks-and-Dolls Era

**Status:** Pre-arc. The era is still in full force.

**The shape:** Gracie is 9 and currently runs a baby doll administration with rock-collection portfolios. At some point she'll age out — not into being agreeable, but into being a kid who runs *things* instead of dolls. Student council, a passion project, something. The arc covers the transition: the moment a doll gets put down for the last time, the new obsession that takes its place, what it means about who she's becoming.

**Where we are:** Pre-arc. Dolls and rocks are at peak. The blog has lore now — doll hospital, doll ICU, dolphin attack. Letting that lore deepen further makes the eventual transition land.

**Open threads:**
- A first sign she's outgrowing it (a doll doesn't make it back into bed one night, a rock collection she stops adding to)
- The new obsession when it appears
- Emily's mixed feelings — relief at less mess, grief at the kid getting older
- The day Emily realizes she hasn't seen the dolls in a week

**How it pays off:** When the moment comes, it's a real post. Probably a Lighter — Field Notes or Observation. Doesn't need to be dramatic. Just noticed.

**What to avoid:** Forcing it. Gracie ages out when she ages out. Emily can't accelerate it for blog purposes, and the writer shouldn't either.

### ARC 3: Emily Becoming a Grandmother

**Status:** Plausible-but-not-yet. Doug is 22 and married to Sherry.

**The shape:** Doug and Sherry are young and in a phase where pregnancy is plausible at any time. When it happens — and it probably will, in the next year or two — Emily becomes a grandmother in her early 40s while still raising a 9- and 10-year-old. That's a real and specific kind of life situation worth writing about.

**Where we are:** Doug and Sherry exist in the world. Have appeared lightly. Not yet a focal couple.

**Open threads:**
- Doug and Sherry need to be *people* before they can be a story — show them a few times in normal contexts (Sunday dinner, holiday) so the announcement, when it comes, lands on a base
- The announcement itself
- The pregnancy itself, which Emily processes through a very specific lens (she has been *pregnant* recently enough to remember it well)
- Becoming a grandmother. The first visit. Holding a baby that is *somehow* hers and not hers.
- Joey and Gracie becoming an aunt and uncle to a baby. They're not far enough apart in age for this to be normal.

**How it pays off:** The announcement and the birth are anchor posts. The texture in between is thread work — Sherry needs to exist as a person.

**What to avoid:** Pushing the timeline. Doug and Sherry have their own lives and timeline. The arc waits until the moment is real.

### ARC 4: The Michelle Friendship and the Comparison Wound

**Status:** Active and ongoing.

**The shape:** Michelle is calm, structured, kind — and Emily compares herself and loses, even though she knows Michelle's life is genuinely harder. The two-layer guilt: feeling small next to her, and feeling small for feeling small. The arc isn't a *resolution* of the wound (some wounds don't resolve, they just become known). It's the deepening texture of the friendship — moments where the comparison sharpens, moments where it softens, the long quiet way friendships actually work.

**Where we are:** Mostly established. Game nights are at Michelle's house. Michelle's son is non-verbal autistic, 6-7. Emily and Joey go to her. The straightening-while-visiting habit follows Michelle when she comes to Emily's.

**Open threads:**
- A moment Emily is genuinely envious and admits it on the page
- A moment Michelle is genuinely envious of Emily (her kids' regulation, her ability to host, her freedom) and Emily catches it
- A scene where Emily helps Michelle with something Michelle is bad at — the equation tilts the other way for once
- The boy gets named when the moment makes naming him feel natural
- Michelle's husband enters the world more (currently nameless)

**How it pays off:** Not as a single arc-resolving post. As an accumulating texture. Probably the most *literary* arc available — character-driven, slow, real.

**What to avoid:** Letting Michelle become a comic foil. She's not. She's a friend Emily loves and quietly measures herself against, and the comedy lives at Emily's expense, not Michelle's.

### ARC 5: Lucas Finding Out How Big the Blog Is Getting

**Status:** Pre-arc. Lucas hasn't engaged.

**The shape:** Right now Lucas thinks the blog is "Emily's thing she does on her laptop." He hasn't sat down and read it. The arc is what happens when that breaks — somebody at church mentions it, an affiliate check shows up larger than expected, a reader sees Emily in public, Lucas reads a post that's about him. Multiple possible triggers. Each version plays differently.

**Where we are:** Pre-arc. The fact of his non-engagement is canon and currently unspoken on the blog itself. Worth being careful here — Emily writing *about* Lucas not knowing about the blog *on the blog* is meta and fragile.

**Open threads:**
- A first event that signals the gap — Emily reads a comment, Lucas walks in, she closes the laptop
- Someone outside the family naming the blog to Lucas's face
- Lucas reading one specific post and what he says about it
- The conversation about money, when affiliate revenue becomes real money
- A version where he reads a post about himself and the post is *kind* (because the writer of this blog is fundamentally kind to her husband) and he's quietly moved
- A version where he reads one and it stings — a real fight, briefly, and then a real conversation

**How it pays off:** This is probably a multi-post arc when it happens — a setup post, a confrontation post, a resolution post — spread over weeks. Anchor-slot territory.

**What to avoid:** Premature winking at the camera. Don't write posts that are *about* Lucas not knowing about the blog. Just keep writing. The arc starts when the world interrupts.

### ARC 6: The Shared-Vehicle Situation

**Status:** Active constraint. Hasn't been a focal post yet.

**The shape:** Lucas and Emily share one vehicle. This is the kind of practical reality that shapes every week — Emily can't easily visit her sick mom, can't easily take the kids somewhere on a whim, has to negotiate every errand. The arc is either *toward getting a second car* (months or years of saving and conversations) or *staying as one car indefinitely and what that means*. Either version is honest.

**Where we are:** Established as fact in the Bible. Hasn't been used much on the page. Easy to weave in as texture.

**Open threads:**
- A specific moment where the one-car constraint shapes a decision (Emily can't go to her mom's, Lucas takes the kids somewhere because it's already in motion, two errands that have to be combined)
- A conversation about whether to get a second car
- The conversation Emily and Lucas have about money, which connects to Arc 5 (when affiliate revenue becomes real money, the second car becomes real)
- The day they buy the second car, if that's where it goes
- The version where they decide they're fine with one — that decision *is* a story too

**How it pays off:** Either as a real moment (they get a second car) or as a quiet accepted fact that keeps showing up as texture forever.

**What to avoid:** Making it sound like deprivation. They have one car because they've decided to. It's a constraint, not a hardship. The writing should respect that.

### ARC 7: The Joey ↔ Lucas Gentle Drift

**Status:** Active and quiet. The most fragile arc on the list.

**The shape:** Joey is closer to Emily because Emily is more present. Lucas loves him but is always slightly catching up. There's a small soft distance between them — not breakage, just pattern. By 13, this pattern has consequences. The arc is *Lucas noticing and trying* — small efforts, some that work, some that don't. The eventual payoff is a stronger Lucas-Joey relationship by the time Joey is a teenager. That's two to three years of real-life timeline.

**Where we are:** A fact in the Bible that the blog hasn't named. Lucas feels the distance. Joey doesn't quite know it's there.

**Open threads:**
- An attempt — Lucas tries to do something with Joey alone, succeeds or doesn't
- A moment Emily watches them interact and notices the distance, or notices that the distance just shrank by an inch
- A *thing* that becomes their thing — fishing, soldering, building, a video game series, a podcast, anything
- A conversation between Lucas and Emily about the gap, which Emily probably initiates carefully
- The first time Joey asks for Lucas instead of Emily

**How it pays off:** This is the most rewarding arc to write well and the easiest to write badly. The *temptation* will be to write it as "Lucas absent dad redeems himself" — that's wrong. Lucas isn't absent. He's tired and behind, and he's quietly fighting his own father stuff (see Bible). The arc is closer to *Lucas trying without making it a project*, and Emily watching and silently rooting and occasionally helping.

**What to avoid:** Making Lucas the bad guy. He's not. The drift is *normal* and *nobody's fault* and *fixable* — and the blog needs to honor all three of those at once. Also avoid making Joey aware of the distance as a problem he needs to fix. He's 10. He doesn't know.

## ACTIVE THREADS

Threads don't have plots. They're the constant texture under the writing. They get *touched* (a line, a reference, a moment), not *featured*.

### THREAD A: Faith

The ground under Emily, not a feature of her week. Touches: a line of scripture in the kitchen window, a prayer half-thought between two tasks, a Sunday morning detail that's about church being where she actually is and not just where Lucas works. **Avoid:** preaching, theological essays, anything that turns Emily's faith into a *topic*. It's a way of being, not a subject.

### THREAD B: Emily's Parents

Mom is sick, in town, critical. Dad is the warm one and is currently her caregiver. Emily probably calls her dad more than her mom. Touches: a phone call mid-task, a drive over, a casserole carried, a specific worry about her dad burning out. **Avoid:** making her mom's illness into the subject of a post. The thread is steady, low-volume background — the way real life is.

### THREAD C: Marie After Widowhood

Marie is recently widowed (her difficult second husband). She's lonely, figuring out who she is now, and shows up with a van of "must-haves" because that's how she knows to love. Touches: a craft project for Gracie, groceries Emily didn't know she needed, a sentence that lands sideways and stays with Emily. **Avoid:** the comic mother-in-law trope. Marie is a grandma who's grieving, not a punchline.

### THREAD D: Doug and Cathy (and Sherry and Joe)

Lucas's biological older kids and their spouses. Family dinners. Holidays. Big news arrives here. Touches: a name in a sentence, a Sunday afternoon stop-by, a text that comes through during something else. **Avoid:** writing them as *Lucas's kids from a previous marriage* in a way that flattens them. They're family. Emily thinks of them as hers.

### THREAD E: Lucas's Bio Dad

Left when Lucas was young. May or may not be alive. Never appears. **Touches very rarely** — a single line in a single post over years. This is the *quiet question, never a closed door* under Lucas's whole life. It earns its weight by almost never being mentioned. If the blog mentions it twice in a year, that's two times too many.

### THREAD F: The Estranged Bio Mom

Doug and Cathy's biological mother, Lucas's ex-wife. Estranged. Doesn't appear. Touches even more rarely than Thread E — possibly never on the page. It's the absence that explains why Lucas and Emily are careful with the older kids. Writers know about it. Readers don't need to.

### THREAD G: Lance

Joey's best friend. Currently mentioned once across 60 posts; needs to become a recurring presence the way Faith and Hope are. Touches: showing up after school, staying for dinner, being at the kitchen table building with Joey while the girls wreak havoc upstairs, his name in passing as part of Joey's normal week. **One writer decision needed early:** Lance's basic register — goofy, serious, quiet, loud. Pick one and stick with it before he starts appearing, or details will contradict each other later. **Avoid:** inventing his family on the fly. He's a kid in the world; he doesn't need a backstory unless one becomes relevant.

## HOW TO USE ARCS DURING POST GENERATION

When generating a post idea (anchor or lighter), check the arcs file. Ask:

1. **Are any arcs ready for a beat?** — meaning, is there an open thread that's been planted long enough that paying it off would land? If yes, that's a real post idea.
2. **Could this post touch a thread?** — meaning, even if the post is about something else (a kitchen drawer, a 15-minute strike), could it organically include a phone call to Dad, a Marie drop-by, a Sherry text? Threads should appear constantly.
3. **Does this post advance an arc unintentionally?** — sometimes a post about something else accidentally moves an arc. That's fine. Note it in the arc's "where we are" section after publishing.
