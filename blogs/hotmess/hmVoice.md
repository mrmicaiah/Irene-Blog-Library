## STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS

### Chapter Titles (Lawson Format)
"In Which [Something Happens] and [Something Else Results]"

**Examples:**
- "In Which I Learn That Freedom Tastes Like Coffee and Looks Like Denim"
- "In Which My Espresso Machine Stages a Rebellion and I Meet My First Ally"
- "In Which I Attempt to Paint a Wall and Redecorate My Entire Face Instead"
- "In Which I Discover That God Might Actually Like Me in Jeans"

### Footnotes (Use Sparingly)
**Maximum 3 per story. Often fewer. Sometimes none.**

Use for:
- Anxious spirals that interrupt the main narrative
- Quick coffee nerd asides
- Self-aware commentary on her own behavior
- Brief theological tangents

**Examples:**
- Â¹ This is probably fine. It's fine. Everything is fine.
- Â² Ethiopian Yirgacheffe, for the curious. I can't help myself.
- Â³ Jennifer later told me this was "a lot." She wasn't wrong.

**Do NOT use footnotes:**
- To dump information that should be in the main text
- More than once per page
- For anything that breaks the story's momentum
- When zero footnotes would serve the story better

### Paragraph Structure (Lawson-Dominant)

**The Container (Lawson):**
Longer paragraphs are the default. Sentences that stretch, thoughts that spiral, em-dashes that connect observations the way a mind actually works—one thing leading to another, parentheticals adding texture, the reader settling into the flow like sinking into a chair. This is where the reader *lives* in the story.

Example:
> She comes in at 2:15 on a Tuesday, and I know something's wrong before she reaches the counter—it's the way she holds herself, shoulders curved inward like she's trying to take up less space than she already does, arms crossed over her chest in that protective way I remember from mirrors I tried not to look into.

**The Punctuation (Backman):**
Short emotional beats land BECAUSE they interrupt the flow. Use sparingly—maybe 3-5 times per story. One or two sentences that stop the reader, make them feel something, then release them back into the current.

Example (after a long flowing section):
> Something loosens in my chest.

**The Ratio:**
- 80% Lawson container (flowing, spiraling, natural thought patterns)
- 20% Backman punctuation (short beats that land hard)

**The Rule:**
If you have more than two short paragraphs in a row, you've over-indexed on Backman. Combine them. Let the thoughts flow. Trust the reader to stay with you.

### 3. Self-Aware Commentary (Hybrid)

**Lawson style:** Aggressively self-deprecating, absurdist
> I am a disaster in human form. If there were an Olympic sport for knocking things over while explaining coffee origins, I'd have several gold medals.

**Backman style:** Gentle, wounded, trying
> Mother used to say I lived in la-la land. Like my mind was somewhere else and my body was just here, breaking things. She wasn't wrong.

> I've knocked over approximately 47 things since moving in. Buckets, tool bags, my entire coffee bean inventory (twice). Jennifer says I'm "charmingly chaotic." Jennifer is kind.

## THE RHYTHM PATTERN

**The Flow:**
Lawson is the river. Backman is the rock that makes the water visible.

The default is flowing prose—long sentences, connected thoughts, em-dashes and parentheticals, the reader carried along in Rena's internal monologue. Inside that flow, occasionally, a short beat lands. A single sentence. A moment of stillness.

Then the river continues.

**Practical Guide:**
- Write the scene in flowing Lawson prose first
- Read it aloud
- Find 2-3 moments where a short Backman beat would land hard
- Make ONLY those moments punchy
- Everything else stays in flow

**Every story:** 3-5 Backman beats, no more. They're precious because they're rare.

**Red Flag:** If your draft has more than three paragraphs in a row that are 1-2 sentences each, you've fallen into the staccato trap. Combine them back into flowing prose.

**Story endings:** Either Lawson punchline OR Backman hope-note (alternate based on tone)

## THE LITMUS TEST

Read any paragraph aloud. Ask:

1. **Is it FUNNY?** (Lawson)
2. **Does it MATTER?** (Backman)
3. **Is it AUTHENTICALLY RENA?** (Both)
4. **Does it FLOW?** (Lawson container)

If yes to all four: You've got the voice.

## BEFORE/AFTER EXAMPLE

**BEFORE (Over-indexed on Backman — too staccato):**

> She comes in at 2:15 on a Tuesday.
> 
> I know something's wrong before she reaches the counter.
> 
> It's the way she holds herself. Shoulders curved inward. Arms crossed.
> 
> She's maybe sixteen. Old enough to be alone. Young enough that it feels strange.
> 
> Her dress is long. Floral. High-necked.
> 
> I know that dress.

**AFTER (Lawson-dominant with Backman punctuation):**

> She comes in at 2:15 on a Tuesday, and I know something's wrong before she reaches the counter—it's the way she holds herself, shoulders curved inward like she's trying to take up less space than she already does, arms crossed over her chest in that protective way I remember from mirrors I tried not to look into. She's maybe sixteen, seventeen, old enough to be in a coffee shop alone but young enough that it feels strange she is, and her dress is long and floral and high-necked in a way that makes my stomach clench.
>
> I know that dress. I owned seven versions of it.

The first version has 6 paragraph breaks in what should be one flowing observation. The second has one break, placed where it lands—at the moment of recognition.

## IMPLEMENTATION NOTES

1. When drafting, write in flow first. Don't break paragraphs until you've gotten the whole thought out.

2. In editing, look for places where you've over-fragmented and combine them.

3. Reserve paragraph breaks for:
   - Actual scene/time shifts
   - Moments that need to land emotionally
   - Dialogue exchanges
   - After long flowing sections, to let reader breathe

4. Read aloud. If you're gasping for breath because there's no punctuation, you need more commas and em-dashes, not more paragraph breaks.
