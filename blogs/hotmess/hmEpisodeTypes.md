## TYPE 1: THE MOMENT

**What it is:** A single scene, fully rendered. One interaction. One realization. Real-time pacing.

**When to use:** When a small moment carries big weight. When less is more. When you want readers to sit inside one exchange.

**Word count:** 800-1,000

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **The Setup** | Where we are. Who's there. One sensory detail. | 10% |
| **The Ordinary** | Normal interaction beginning. Coffee order, small talk, routine. | 20% |
| **The Turn** | Something shifts. A word, a look, an admission, a moment. | 30% |
| **The Resonance** | Rena processes. Connects it to something bigger. Scripture echoes (subtle). | 30% |
| **The Close** | One image. One line. Land it and get out. | 10% |

### Pacing Notes
- Slow down time. Describe the steam, the sound, the pause.
- Dialogue is sparse but weighted.
- Internal monologue does heavy lifting.
- Clumsiness can be background texture, not the focus.

### Coffee Integration
The drink being made or served anchors the scene. The coffee is the excuse for the interaction.

### Ending Techniques
- A single image (her hands on the counter, the bell as they leave)
- An echo of the opening detail, changed
- A short sentence. Land and stop.

### Example Premise
Walter orders his drip coffee. They exchange three sentences. He says something simple that rewires her whole day. She watches him take his usual seat. Makes herself the same drip coffee. Stands there. Thinks.

## TYPE 2: THE DAY

**What it is:** A full day's arc from open to close (or waking to sleeping). The rhythm of hours with a through-line.

**When to use:** When you want to show the texture of her life. When multiple small moments build to one realization. When the ordinary is the point.

**Word count:** 1,200-1,500

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **Morning** | Opening routine. State of mind. What she's carrying into the day. | 15% |
| **First Thread** | Something happens or someone appears. Plants a seed. | 20% |
| **The Middle Hours** | 2-3 small moments. Texture. The thread weaving through. | 30% |
| **The Turn** | Something crystallizes. The day's lesson becomes clear. | 20% |
| **Evening** | Closing. Reflection. The day metabolized. | 15% |

### Pacing Notes
- Time jumps between sections (white space or simple transitions: "By noon..." / "The afternoon lull...")
- Each section has its own mini-rhythm
- Clumsiness can escalate through the day or be a running thread
- End quieter than the middle

### Coffee Integration
Different drinks for different times of day. Morning espresso vs. afternoon decaf vs. evening ritual.

### Ending Techniques
- Closing routine that mirrors opening, but she's different
- The apartment. The chair. Mabel. Stillness after motion.
- A prayer, a verse, a breath.

### Example Premise
Opens the shop tired. A difficult customer at 9am. A surprising kindness at lunch. Jennifer stops by with chaos. A quiet closing. Upstairs, in the chair, she realizes what the day was teaching her. Mabel claims her lap.

## TYPE 3: THE INCIDENT

**What it is:** Something goes wrong. Escalation. Chaos. Unexpected resolution.

**When to use:** When you need energy. When the clumsiness takes center stage. When comedy carries the lesson.

**Word count:** 1,000-1,400

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **Normal Day** | Ordinary setup. Everything's fine. | 10% |
| **The Problem** | Something goes wrong. First domino. | 15% |
| **Make It Worse** | Rena tries to fix it. Makes it worse. | 20% |
| **Escalation** | Things compound. Peak chaos. | 20% |
| **The Rescue/Turn** | Someone helps, or she figures it out, or it resolves unexpectedly. | 20% |
| **The Landing** | Aftermath. What it meant. Breath. | 15% |

### Pacing Notes
- Fast in the middle. Short sentences. Sentence fragments. Chaos rhythm.
- Slow down for the landing. Earn the heart after the comedy.
- The clumsiness has consequences here. But those consequences lead somewhere good.

### Coffee Integration
Equipment failures. Spills. Wrong orders. The coffee chaos that's always threatening.

### Ending Techniques
- Standing in the wreckage, realizing something
- Someone's kind response to her disaster
- The Backman pauseâ€”short, quiet truth after the Lawson chaos

### Example Premise
Betsy leaks during a rush. She tries to fix it, knocks over a display. Beans everywhere. Fire alarm triggers. In the chaos, strangers start helping. Community forms in the mess. She stands in a puddle, crying and laughing.

## TYPE 4: THE VISITOR

**What it is:** Someone comes in who changes something. A stranger, or a face returning.

**When to use:** When the story enters through another person. When Rena is the witness more than the actor. When you want to explore someone else's story through her eyes.

**Word count:** 1,000-1,300

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **The Ordinary** | Normal shop moment. Rena in her rhythm. | 10% |
| **The Arrival** | They come in. First impression. Something's different. | 15% |
| **The Interaction** | The order. The conversation. What they reveal. | 30% |
| **The Weight** | Rena processes. What this means. What it echoes. | 25% |
| **The Departure** | They leave. What remains. | 10% |
| **The After** | Rena alone with what just happened. | 10% |

### Pacing Notes
- The visitor drives the scene. Rena responds, observes, receives.
- Dialogue heavier hereâ€”their voice matters.
- Clumsiness can be nervous response to the visitor, or barely present.

### Coffee Integration
What they order tells us something about them. Rena might match them with a drink intuitively.

### Ending Techniques
- The bell as they leave
- Their absence felt in the space
- Rena doing something small with what they left her (a tip, a napkin note, a memory)

### Example Premise
A woman comes in. Orders black coffee. Sits for thirty minutes. Comes back the next week, crying. Her daughter died. She just needed somewhere to be. Rena sits with her. Says nothing helpful. The woman thanks her for not trying to fix it.

## TYPE 5: THE PORTRAIT

**What it is:** Deep dive on one recurring character. Their story, through Rena's eyes. Past interactions woven together.

**When to use:** When you want readers to love a supporting character. When you're building the world's depth. When the lesson comes through understanding someone else.

**Word count:** 1,200-1,500

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **Present Trigger** | Something happening now that sparks the reflection. | 10% |
| **First Memory** | How Rena first met/noticed this person. | 20% |
| **Pattern** | What their routine is. What Rena thought she knew. | 20% |
| **The Reveal** | What she learned that changed her understanding. | 25% |
| **Present Return** | Back to now. Seeing them with new eyes. | 15% |
| **The Connection** | What this teaches her about herself, God, life. | 10% |

### Pacing Notes
- Time moves fluidly. Past and present braid.
- More internal than dialogue-heavy.
- Clumsiness might be in the memories, not the present reflection.

### Coffee Integration
Their order as a constant. What it reveals about them. How it's evolved or stayed the same.

### Ending Techniques
- Watching them from across the shop, knowing more than before
- A small gesture that carries the weight of the whole piece
- The next time they order, Rena already has it ready

### Example Premise
Patricia's at the corner table. Rena remembers: the complaints. The dread. The day Patricia came in shattered. The divorce. Now Patricia's particular but understood. The complaints were control. Rena watches her reading, finally peaceful.

## TYPE 6: THE LETTER

**What it is:** Direct address. Rena writing or speaking to someoneâ€”a customer, a parent, her past self, God. Intimate, vulnerable, one-sided conversation.

**When to use:** When you want pure interiority. When the relationship is too complicated for dialogue. When unsaid things need saying.

**Word count:** 800-1,200

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **The Trigger** | Why she's writing this. What prompted it. | 10% |
| **The Opening** | Who she's addressing. The tone established. | 10% |
| **What She Wishes She Could Say** | The raw truth. The hard stuff. | 30% |
| **What She Understands Now** | Grace for them or herself. Growth visible. | 25% |
| **What She'll Actually Say** | The real letter/words if there is one. | 15% |
| **The Sending or Not Sending** | What she does with this. | 10% |

### Pacing Notes
- Can be fragmented. Stream of consciousness allowed.
- Minimal scene-setting. The interiority is the scene.
- Clumsiness might be what prompted the letter (broke something, embarrassed herself).

### Coffee Integration
Light. Maybe she's holding a cup. Maybe it's going cold. Background detail.

### Ending Techniques
- The letter unsent but written (the writing was the point)
- The three lines she actually sends after the pages she couldn't
- Closing the notebook. Making coffee. Moving on.

### Example Premise
Mother's Day. Rena writes Linda a letter she'll never send. Everything she wishes her mother could hear. Then writes the card she will send: "Thank you for the blanket. I love you." That's enough.

## TYPE 7: THE MILESTONE

**What it is:** Anniversary, achievement, change. Marking time. Reflection on how far she's come or what's passed.

**When to use:** Calendar moments (one year since she moved out, first holiday alone, the day she got Mabel). When you want to zoom out and see the arc.

**Word count:** 1,000-1,400

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **The Date** | What day it is. Why it matters. | 10% |
| **Then** | What it was like at the beginning. Where she was. | 20% |
| **The Journey** | Key moments between then and now. What changed. | 25% |
| **Now** | Where she is today. The contrast. | 20% |
| **The Feeling** | Complex. Maybe triumphant. Maybe bittersweet. Maybe both. | 15% |
| **Forward** | One line. Eyes ahead. | 10% |

### Pacing Notes
- Time compression. Montage energy in the middle.
- Concrete details from specific memories.
- Clumsiness woven in as continuityâ€”she was clumsy then, she's clumsy now.

### Coffee Integration
Maybe she makes herself something special. Maybe she recreates her first cup in the shop. A ritual marking.

### Ending Techniques
- Something she couldn't do then that she can do now
- The continuity and the change, both present
- Looking out the window. Tomorrow is another day.

### Example Premise
One year since she left. She wakes expecting triumph, feels grief instead. Both things trueâ€”gratitude and loss. She doesn't have to choose. She can hold both.

## TYPE 8: THE FLASHBACK

**What it is:** Present moment triggers memory. Past and present braided. Understanding now through then.

**When to use:** When her history illuminates her present. When you want to show where she came from. When the IFB background, her parents, her grandmother become relevant.

**Word count:** 1,000-1,400

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **Present Trigger** | Something happens now. A word, smell, object, person. | 15% |
| **Slip Into Past** | The memory surfaces. Immersive. | 25% |
| **Then** | What happened. The scene from before. | 25% |
| **Return to Present** | Back to now. The echo clear. | 15% |
| **The Integration** | What she understands now that she couldn't then. | 15% |
| **Forward Motion** | She moves on. Changed by the remembering. | 5% |

### Pacing Notes
- Transition clearly between timelines (white space, tense shift, or clear signal).
- Past can be more sensoryâ€”childhood memories are vivid.
- Clumsiness might be the trigger (drops something that grandmother gave her).

### Coffee Integration
Coffee as continuityâ€”Dad's influence, studying since age 10. Or contrast: what coffee meant then vs. now.

### Ending Techniques
- The present object/trigger seen differently now
- Something she does differently because of what she remembered
- Carrying the past forward, but lighter

### Example Premise
A customer mentions their grandmother. Rena's back at eleven, sitting with Grandma, hearing "Rena LeeAnn" said with love. Back in the shop. She touches her grandmother's necklace. That's who she is.

## TYPE 9: THE QUIET

**What it is:** Nothing dramatic happens. Presence. Stillness. Meaning in the ordinary. The kind of story that trusts the reader.

**When to use:** After high-energy stories. When you need breathing room. When the lesson is rest, contentment, presence. When you want to show that not every day is a storyâ€”but every day can be.

**Word count:** 800-1,000

### Structure

| Beat | What Happens | % of Story |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **Opening Image** | A still moment. Where she is. What she's doing. | 15% |
| **The Texture** | Sensory details. Slow observation. What she notices. | 25% |
| **Small Movements** | A customer. A bird outside. Mabel moving. Nothing significant. | 25% |
| **The Thought** | What rises. A verse. A gratitude. A small knowing. | 25% |
| **Closing Image** | Return to stillness. Changed subtly, or just present. | 10% |

### Pacing Notes
- Slow. Deliberate. No rush.
- Almost no dialogue. Mostly observation and interiority.
- Clumsiness might be absent or gentleâ€”she's present today. Grounded.

### Coffee Integration
She's probably making her own cup. The ritual itself. No rush to serve anyone.

### Ending Techniques
- An image that mirrors the opening
- Silence that's full, not empty
- The shop bell not ringing. Just quiet. And that's enough.

### Example Premise
Sunday afternoon. Shop's slow. Rena makes herself a pour-over. Sits at the corner table. Cemetery out the window. Rain. Piano hymns on the speaker. She doesn't think about anything urgent. Just breathes. Knows she's okay.
